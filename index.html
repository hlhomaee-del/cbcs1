📘 What Is Medicare?

Medicare is a federal health insurance program administered by CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services). It is NOT income-based — it is based on age (65+) and/or qualifying conditions. It is divided into four parts, each with distinct billing implications.

A
Medicare
Hospital Insurance
Inpatient-oriented coverage
Tap to expand ▼

Covers: Inpatient hospital stays · Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF) after qualifying stay · Hospice · Some home health care · Inpatient psychiatric


Billing Implication: When a patient is admitted (not just observed), Part A applies. Know the difference — observation stays may be Part B.


Cost: Most get premium-free Part A if they paid Medicare taxes long enough. Deductible per benefit period.

B
Medicare
Medical Insurance
Outpatient/physician services
Tap to expand ▼

Covers: Physician/provider visits · Outpatient hospital services · Preventive services · Durable Medical Equipment (DME) · Lab, X-ray, imaging (outpatient) · Ambulance


Billing Implication: Part B pays 80% of approved amount after annual deductible. Patient owes 20% coinsurance — unless supplemental plan covers it.


Cost: Monthly premium required. Late enrollment = permanent penalty.

C
Medicare
Medicare Advantage
Private plan alternative to Original Medicare
Tap to expand ▼

What it is: Private insurers approved by CMS offer plans that bundle Part A + B (usually + D). NOT the same as Medigap.


Billing Implication: Bill the private MA plan — NOT CMS directly. Network rules apply. Prior auth requirements vary by plan.


Key Exam Point: Part C IS NOT a supplement. It REPLACES Original Medicare for enrolled beneficiaries.

D
Medicare
Prescription Drug Plan
Outpatient prescription coverage
Tap to expand ▼

Covers: Outpatient prescription drugs from a pharmacy. Does NOT cover drugs administered in a hospital (Part A) or infused in a physician office (may be Part B).


Billing Implication: Part D claims go to the Prescription Drug Plan — not to Medicare directly. The PDP has its own formulary and cost-sharing.


Key Exam Point: Part D = pharmacy drugs only. In-office injections/infusions = usually Part B.

🚫 Critical Exam Trap

Part C ≠ Supplement. Part C (Medicare Advantage) REPLACES Original Medicare through a private plan. Medigap SUPPLEMENTS Original Medicare. These require completely different billing workflows. Never treat them the same.

⚠️ Inpatient vs. Observation

A patient "admitted" to the hospital = Part A. A patient in the hospital under "observation status" = Part B (outpatient). This distinction affects patient responsibility significantly.

📘 What Is Medicaid?

Medicaid is a joint federal-state program providing health coverage to low-income individuals and families. Unlike Medicare, it IS income-based. Each state administers its own Medicaid program within federal guidelines — so rules, benefits, and eligible populations vary by state. This makes verification critical.

MC
Program
Medicaid
Joint federal-state, income-based
Tap to expand ▼

Who it covers: Low-income adults · Children · Pregnant women · Elderly (may dual-qualify with Medicare) · Disabled individuals meeting income criteria


Billing Implications: Medicaid is always the PAYER OF LAST RESORT. If patient has any other insurance, bill that first. Medicaid has the lowest reimbursement rates.


Verification Why: Medicaid eligibility can change monthly. Never assume — always verify for the date of service.

💡 Revenue-Cycle Lens

Medicaid requires verification at every visit — not just at intake. A claim submitted for a date when the patient's Medicaid had lapsed will be denied. Always confirm eligibility for the specific DOS (Date of Service).

⚠️ Medicare ≠ Medicaid

These are two separate programs. Medicare = federal, age/disability-based. Medicaid = state-administered, income-based. A person CAN have both (dual eligible) — Medicare pays first, Medicaid pays last (payer of last resort).

📘 Medigap (Medicare Supplement Insurance)

Medigap is private insurance that supplements Original Medicare (Parts A and B). It covers cost-sharing gaps — like the 20% Part B coinsurance — that Medicare does not pay. Medigap only works with Original Medicare, NOT with Medicare Advantage (Part C).

MG
Supplemental
Medigap
Fills cost-sharing gaps in Original Medicare
Tap to expand ▼

What it covers: Part A and/or Part B cost-sharing — deductibles, coinsurance, copays (varies by plan letter)


Billing: Bill Medicare first. After Medicare adjudicates, bill Medigap as secondary. Many Medigap claims are cross-over claims — sent automatically.


Critical Point: Medigap + Original Medicare ≠ Medicare Advantage. DO NOT mix these workflows.

📘 TRICARE

TRICARE is the healthcare program for active duty military, veterans, and their families. Administered by the Department of Defense. TRICARE has its own network, rules, and prior authorization requirements. Do not assume civilian insurance rules apply.

TR
Military

Plans include: TRICARE Prime (HMO-like) · TRICARE Select (PPO-like) · TRICARE for Life (Medicare supplement for qualifying retirees)


Billing: Verify TRICARE plan type. TRICARE for Life patients have Medicare primary, TRICARE secondary.

💡 Revenue-Cycle Lens

In billing, "I know the patient has insurance" is not enough. You must know: which type (government vs. private), which plan, what the network rules are, whether prior auth is required, and what the patient's responsibility will be. Each answer changes the workflow.

Higher-Order Thinking: Analyze & Evaluate

PPO plans offer more provider choice — you can see any in-network provider without a referral, and even out-of-network providers (at higher cost). But this flexibility has a price: PPO premiums are generally higher, and the out-of-network benefit means patients may face higher deductibles and coinsurance when they go outside the preferred network. HMOs restrict choice but often have lower premiums and more predictable cost-sharing. From a billing perspective, PPO patients who see out-of-network providers may owe a much larger share — which must be explained before services are rendered.

💡 Billing Implication

The billing office must tell PPO patients: "You can see this out-of-network provider, but your cost will be significantly higher." Failure to explain this creates patient disputes after the claim pays.

Insurance plan names can be misleading. A plan called "Blue Shield PPO Premier" might still require prior authorization for certain services. The word HMO, PPO, or even Medicare does not tell you all the rules — you must verify the specific plan's benefit structure, network requirements, and authorization rules every time. Assuming the rules based on a name is one of the most common front-end errors in billing.

🚫 Denial Risk

A service rendered without verifying the actual rules of the specific plan — just assuming based on the plan type name — is a root cause of authorization denials and network denials. Always verify the actual plan, not just the plan category.

The workflows are fundamentally different. With Original Medicare + Medigap: you bill Medicare first, Medicare adjudicates, then Medigap pays the cost-sharing. With Medicare Advantage: you do NOT bill Medicare directly. You bill the private MA plan, which has its own network, prior auth rules, and fee schedule. Using the wrong workflow results in denials, delayed payment, and administrative rework. This is one of the most tested workflow confusions on the CBCS exam.

Step-by-Step Verification Workflow

Tap each step to see what the office must do and why it matters.

1
Collect Insurance Info
Get insurance card, member ID, group number

Collect the insurance card (both sides), member ID number, group number, payer name, and policyholder information. Verify the patient is the member OR a covered dependent. Missing any of these makes verification impossible.

→
2
Verify Eligibility
Confirm active coverage for DOS

Contact the payer (online portal, phone, or clearinghouse) to confirm the patient has active coverage on the DATE OF SERVICE. A card in hand ≠ active coverage. Policies can lapse, change, or terminate. Medicaid eligibility changes monthly.

→
3
Verify Benefits
Determine what IS and IS NOT covered

Eligibility ≠ Benefits. A patient can be eligible but the specific service may not be covered. Verify: Is this service covered? What are the in-network vs. out-of-network benefit levels? What is the deductible? Has it been met? What is the copay/coinsurance? Are there visit limits? Is prior auth needed?

→
4
Check Network Status
Is the provider in-network?

Determine whether the rendering provider and the facility are in-network for THIS patient's plan. A provider can be in-network for one plan and out-of-network for another, even from the same insurer. Always verify network status for the specific plan.

→
5
Determine Auth Needed
Does this service require prior auth?

Based on the plan rules and the service being requested, determine if prior authorization is required. If yes, obtain it BEFORE the service. A claim for a service that required auth but didn't have it will be denied — even if everything else was done correctly.

→
6
Estimate Patient $
What will the patient owe?

Based on verified benefits, calculate or estimate the patient's responsibility: deductible remaining + copay/coinsurance. Inform the patient BEFORE service — this reduces patient disputes, increases upfront collections, and improves patient satisfaction.

💡 Revenue-Cycle Lens

Every step in this workflow prevents a problem downstream. Skipping step 3 (benefits) means you may render a non-covered service. Skipping step 5 (auth) means a probable denial. Skipping step 6 (patient $) means disputes and bad debt. The front end is the most powerful point in the revenue cycle.

Required Documentation

Insurance Card

Front AND back. Contains: Payer name, member ID, group number, plan type, copay amounts, payer phone, and claims address.

Photo ID

Government-issued ID to verify the patient's identity and match it to the insurance card.

Payer Verification

Phone call, online portal, or clearinghouse real-time eligibility check. A card is NOT sufficient — must confirm active coverage for the date of service.

Secondary Insurance

If the patient has secondary coverage, collect that card too. Coordination of benefits affects billing order and patient balance.

⚠️ Eligibility ≠ Benefits

A patient can be eligible (active member) but the specific service may not be covered under their benefits. Always verify both separately. Eligibility tells you the policy is active. Benefits tell you what the policy will pay for.

Special Situations

⚠️ Self-Pay

Patient has no insurance OR chooses not to use insurance. Verify: Is there truly no coverage? Offer financial counseling. Collect payment arrangement upfront. Self-pay rates may differ from insurance rates.

🚫 Uninsured

No active coverage. Determine: Does the patient qualify for any government program (Medicaid, CHIP)? Is charity care applicable? Document uninsured status for compliance.

⏳ Not Yet Verified

Insurance presented but not yet confirmed active. Do NOT render non-emergency services until verification is complete. Hold the appointment or verify first.

🌐 Out-of-Network

Provider or facility is not in the patient's network. Must advise the patient of their out-of-network cost exposure BEFORE the service. For HMO patients, this may mean NO coverage (except emergencies).

📋 Lesson 2: Referrals, Authorizations & Predeterminations

These four concepts are distinct — and confusing them causes denials. Master the differences here.

  • Read each definition and the comparison cards
  • Complete the workflow simulation
  • Answer all concept-check questions
  • Points available: Up to 80 pts

📤 Referral

A recommendation from a PCP to another provider (specialist) for evaluation or treatment. Required by HMO and some POS plans before specialist visits. FROM the physician. TO the specialist.


Denial risk: Specialist claim without referral in HMO = denial

🔒 Preauthorization / Precertification

Advance approval from the insurance company that a specific service is medically necessary and will be covered. Obtained BEFORE the service. FROM the insurance company.


Denial risk: Service rendered without required prior auth = denial

✅ Authorization

Formal approval number from the insurance company confirming a service has been authorized. Received AFTER requesting preauthorization. Always document the authorization number.


Best practice: Record auth number in the patient chart and claim

📊 Predetermination

A request to the payer to determine how much it will pay for a specific service — BEFORE the service is performed. Unlike preauthorization, it does NOT guarantee payment. Used to estimate patient responsibility for expensive procedures.


Note: Predetermination ≠ preauthorization. Does not guarantee coverage.

🚫 Critical Distinctions

• Referral = physician-to-physician recommendation (required for HMO specialists)
• Preauthorization = insurer approval BEFORE service (required for many procedures)
• Authorization = the approval number received after preauth request
• Predetermination = cost estimate only — NOT approval. Does not guarantee coverage.

💡 Revenue-Cycle Lens

Prior authorization is the single most important pre-service task for preventing denials on high-cost procedures. The process: identify if the service requires auth → submit clinical documentation → receive authorization number → document it → render service → include auth number on the claim. Missing any step = potential denial.
